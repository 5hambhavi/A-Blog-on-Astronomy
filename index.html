The Solar System


What is the Solar System?

The Solar System is the gravitationally bound system of out star, the Sun, and the planets that orbit around it. The planets are further identified as four gas giants, four terrestrial planets and an unknown number of dwarf planets.


Components of the Solar System

Some of the major objects in our Solar System in their fixed order are:



Mercury

Mercury is the smallest and the first planet in our Solar System and is the closest to the Sun. Its one complete revolution around the Sun takes about 88 Earth days, the shortest of all the other planets.


Venus

Venus is the second planet from the Sun in our Solar System. Even though Mercury is the closest to the Sun, Venus is the hottest planet in our Solar System. Its temperature can reach up to 900¬∞F or about 475¬∞C.


Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and is the only celestial body known to consist of life. About 71% of it is made up of water while the other 29% is land and its atmosphere mostly consists of nitrogen and oxygen.


Mars

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun in our Solar System, also referred to as the Red Planet. It is a terrestrial planet with a thin atmosphere, with surface features such as valleys, dunes and polar ice caps.


Jupiter

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest planet in our Solar System. It is a gas giant which primarily consists of hydrogen but helium constitutes one-quarter of its mass and one-tenth of its volume.


Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and is the second largest planet before Jupiter. It is also a gas giant. The planet's most noticeable feature are its rings; mainly made out of ice particles.


Uranus

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun. It is almost the size of Neptune, and is an ice giant. Its atmosphere is similar to Jupiter and Saturn's, consisting of hydrogen and helium.


Neptune

Neptune is the eighth and the last planet from the Sun in our Solar System. It is referred to as one of the two Solar System's ice giants, along with its neighbouring planet, Uranus.


2. Exoplanets


What are exoplanets?

Exoplanets or extrasolar planets are planets that orbit a star other than our sun or can also be defined as planets that are not a part of our solar system. The Greek term 'exo' stands for 'outside'. As these worlds are far, far away from our solar system, it is next to impossible to spot them directly with telescopes. They are so far away from all of humanity that the unit to measure the farness is ly (light years). Astronomers have confirmed more than 4,000 exoplanets exist and are orbiting distant stars, with at least 1,000 more awaiting confirmation. They found the first two exoplanets orbiting a pulsar in 1992. They confirmed the first exoplanet orbiting a sunlike star in 1995.


How do you detect exoplanets?

There are various methods of detecting exoplanets. Two major ones are :

Radial Velocity

This method uses gravity to detect planets. The planet is much smaller than its star and exerts a small gravitational pull on the star as it orbits. When the planet is behind its star, it pulls that star slightly away. When its in front of it, it pulls the star slightly towards us. This makes the star wobble. Astronomers look for this wobbling to find planets. To find this wobble, they use something known as a spectrograph. A spectrograph is like a prism. It splits the light from the star into its component colours making a spectrum. Some of the light gets absorbed as it passes through a star's atmosphere. This produces small, dark lines in the spectrum. As the star moves closer to us, the lines shift towards the blue end of the spectrum. As it shifts away, the lines shift to the red end. Astronomers look for orbiting planets by looking for these back and forth movements of the lines in the spectrum.


Transit photometry

This method uses the concept of shadows to find exoplanets. Around the star, if a planet's orbit is just right, then the planet will regularly pass in front of it's star or transit. When it does this it will block a part of the star's light (like a solar eclipse). It's a small change but is enough to clue astronomers of the presence of an exoplanet around a distant star. If the brightness of the star changes regularly and consistantly, it could mean that a planet is constantly revolving around its star, blocking some part of the star's light each time.

Teegarden b

Teegarden b is an exoplanet found orbiting within the habitable zone of Teegarden's Star, an M-type red dwarf which is around 12 light years away from our solar system. Together with Teegarden's Star c, GJ 1061 c, GJ 1061 d, Luyten b and Tau Ceti e, it is the fourth-closest potentially habitable exoplanet as of April 2020.


Gliese 667 Cc

Gliese 667 Cc (also known as GJ 667Cc, HR 6426Cc, or HD 156384Cc) is an exoplanet orbiting within the habitable zone of the red dwarf star Gliese 667 C, which is a member of the Gliese 667 triple star system, approximately 23.62 light-years (7.24 parsecs; 223.5 trillion kilometres) away in the constellation of Scorpius. The exoplanet was found by using the radial velocity method, from radial-velocity measurements via observation of Doppler shifts in the spectrum of the planet's parent star.


Proxima Centauri b

Proxima Centauri b (or Proxima b) is an exoplanet in the constellation of Centaurus orbiting in the habitable zone of the red dwarf star Proxima Centauri, which is the closest star to the Sun and part of the triple star system Alpha Centauri. It is approximately 4.2 ly away from Earth making it, Proxima Centauri c, and Proxima Centauri d the closest known exoplanets to the Solar System.


CoRoT-7b

CoRoT-7b (previously named CoRoT-Exo-7b) is an exoplanet orbiting the star CoRoT-7 in the constellation of Monoceros, 489 ly from Earth. It was first detected using the transit photometry method by the French-led CoRoT mission and reported in February 2009. Until the announcement of Kepler-10b in January 2011, it was the smallest exoplanet to have its diameter measured, at 1.58 times that of the Earth and the first potential extrasolar terrestrial planet to be found. The exoplanet has a very short period of revolution, revolving around its host star in about 20 hours.


Gliese 1214 b

Gliese 1214 b (also known as GJ 1214 b) is an exoplanet that orbits the star Gliese 1214, and was discovered in December 2009. Its parent star is 48 ly from the Sun, in the constellation Ophiuchus. GJ 1214 b is known for being an ocean planet, as water cover 10% of the planet's mass. For this reason, scientists have nicknamed the planet as "the waterworld".


3. Constellations


What are constellations?

Constellations are a group of stars together which form a pattern in the night sky. The latin word constellacio means a set of stars.


Examples of constellations

Some of the major constellations are :


Centaurus

Centaurus constellation is located in the southern hemisphere. It is one of the Greek constellations, discovered by the Greek astronomer and mathematician Ptolemy in the 2nd century, but it was mentioned in earlier Greek texts by the astronomer Eudoxus and the poet Aratus. Centaurus consists two of the top ten brightest stars in the night sky; Alpha Centauri and Beta Centauri. It is also home to Centaurus A, one of the brightest galaxies, and to the cluster of Omega Centauri. The famous Blue Planetary nebula, also known as the Southerner, and the Boomerang Nebula are also located in Centaurus.


Ophiuchus

Ophiuchus constellation lies in the southern sky, on the celestial equator. Its name means ‚Äúthe serpent bearer‚Äù in Greek. The constellation is associated with the figure of Asclepius, the famous healer in Greek mythology. It was one of the constellations first discovered by the Greek astronomer and mathematician Ptolemy in the 2nd century. It is also known by its Latin name, Serpentarius. Ophiuchus is generally depicted as a man holding a snake, represented by the neighboring constellation Serpens, which is divided into two parts by Ophiuchus: Serpens Caput, the snake's head, and Serpens Cauda, the snake's tail. The snake is usually depicted coiled around his waist.


Ursa Major

Ursa Major constellation lies in the northern celestial hemisphere. Its name means ‚Äúthe great bear,‚Äù or ‚Äúthe larger bear,‚Äù in Latin. The smaller bear is represented by Ursa Minor. It is the largest northern constellation and third largest constellation in the sky. Its brightest stars form the Big Dipper, one of the most recognizable shapes in the night sky also known as the Plough. Ursa Major is well known in most world cultures and associated with a number of myths. It was discovered by the Greek astronomer and mathematcian Ptolemy in the 2nd century. In Greek mythology, it is associated with Callisto, a nymph who was turned into a bear by Zeus' jealous wife Hera.


Ursa Minor

Ursa Minor constellation lies in the northern sky. The constellation's name means ‚Äúthe smaller bear,‚Äù or ‚Äúthe lesser bear,‚Äù in Latin. The Great Bear constellation is represented by its larger neighbor Ursa Major. It was first catalogued by the Greek astronomer Ptolemy in the 2nd century. It is easy to recognize because it contains the famous Little Dipper asterism. Ursa Minor is also notable for marking the location of the north celestial pole, as it is home to Polaris, the North Star, which is located at the end of the dipper's handle.


Orion

Orion constellation is one of the brightest known constellations in the night sky. It lies on the celestial equator. It has been known since ancient times. The constellation is also known as the Hunter, as it is associated with one in Greek mythology. It represents the mythical hunter Orion, who is often depicted in star maps as either facing the charge of Taurus, the bull, pursuing the Pleiades sisters, represented by the famous open cluster, or chasing after the hare (constellation of Lepus) with his two hunting dogs, represented by the nearby constellations Canis Major and Canis Minor.


Andromeda

Andromeda constellation is located in the northern sky, between Cassiopeia's W asterism and the Great Square of Pegasus. It is one of the 48 Greek constellations, found by Ptolemy . The constellation was named after the mythical princess Andromeda, the daughter of Queen Cassiopeia and wife of the Greek hero Perseus. It is also known as the Chained Maiden, Persea (wife of Perseus), or Cepheis (daughter of Cepheus).


Scorpius

Scorpius constellation lies in the southern sky. It represents the scorpion and is associated with the story of Orion in Greek mythology. It is one of the zodiac constellations, first found by the Greek astronomer Ptolemy in the 2nd century. It is located between Libra to the west and Sagittarius to the east.


Phoenix

Phoenix constellation lies in the southern sky. It was named after the phoenix, the mythical bird that rises from its own ashes. The constellation was originally introduced by the Dutch-Flemish astronomer and cartographer Petrus Plancius from the observations of the Dutch navigators Frederick Houtman and Pieter Dirkszoon Keyser in the late 16th century. It is a small constellation, but it's the largest among the 12 constellations created and named by Plancius. It was first depicted on his globe in 1598 and later appeared in Johann Bayer's atlas Uranometria in 1603.


Monoceros

Monoceros constellation lies in the northern sky, on the celestial equator. In Latin, Monoceros means "unicorn". This constellation was introduced by the Dutch astronomer and cartographer Petrus Plancius from the observations of Dutch navigators in the 17th century.It represents the mythical single-horned, horse-like creature. Monoceros is a relatively faint constellation, containing only a few fourth magnitude stars, but is still home to several notable stars: the famous variables S Monocerotis, R Monocerotis, and V838 Monocerotis, Plaskett's Star, which is one of the most massive binary stars known, and the triple star Beta Monocerotis.

5. Stars


What are stars?

A star us a celestial object which emits light and comprises a luminous spheroid of plasma held together by its gravity. Most of the prominent stars have been categorised into constellations and asterisms, whereas the brightest stars have proper names.

UY Scuti

UY Scuti is a red supergiant star in the constellation of Scutum. It is considered to be one of the biggest known stars, with a maximum brightness of magnitude 8.29 and minimum of magnitude 10.56. It has a radius of 7.94 AU, thus a volume almost 5 billion times of our Sun. The distance from Earth to UY Scuti is approximately 9,500 ly.


VY Canis Majoris

VY Canis Majoris is an oxygen-rich(O-rich) red hypergiant star in the constellation of Canis Major, which is about 3,900 ly from us and the Solar System. It also is a very big star, one of the most luminous stars and massive red supergiants in the Milky Way galaxy.


Proxima Centauri

Proxima Centauri is a small, low-mass, red dwarf star located about 4.24 ly away from the Sun (nearest star to the Sun that is outside of our Solar System) in the constellation of Centaurus. It is a member of the Alpha Centauri system of stars which was discovered in 1915 by Robert Innes.


Arcturus

Arcturus is the brightest star in the northern constellation of Bo√∂tes and is the fourth-brightest star in the night sky. It is about 36.7 ly away from the Sun.


Betelgeuse

Betelgeuse is the tenth-brightest star in the night sky, lying in the constellation of Orion and is classified as a red supergiant. For some reasons, the distance from it to the Sun is quite difficult to measure but the estimates are around 500-600 ly.


Rigel

Rigel also lies in the constellation of Orion, but it is a blue supergiant instead of a red one. The distance to it from Earth is approximately 860 ly. It is calculated to be anywhere between 61,500 to 363,000 times as luminous as the Sun and 18 to 24 times as massive.
